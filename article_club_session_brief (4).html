What the article is about

The paradox at the heart of Mexico's economy

Mexico sits next to the world's largest economy, benefits from a sweeping trade agreement, and has attracted record foreign investment as companies move supply chains away from China. The peso was one of the world's best-performing currencies in 2025. By every structural measure, Mexico should be booming.

Instead, it has grown at under 1% per year for seven years. More than half its workforce operates in the informal economy — paying no taxes, receiving no state benefits, and contributing to a productivity trap that successive governments have failed to break. This week's Economist analysis asks a direct question: what exactly is broken, and why is it so hard to fix?

Key themes to look for

Three threads running through the article

The informality trap. Over half of Mexico's workers are outside the formal economy — not paying taxes, not accruing pension rights, not building documented skills. This is not just a welfare problem. It is a productivity problem, a fiscal problem, and a political problem. The article examines why it is so persistent and what would have to change to reverse it.

The nearshoring paradox. The relocation of manufacturing from China to Mexico has generated headlines and investment flows. But nearshoring is concentrated in a narrow corridor of northern states. Most of Mexico — and most Mexican workers — have seen little benefit. The article asks whether nearshoring can transform the broader economy, or whether structural barriers will limit its reach.

The institutional question. Under both former President López Obrador and current President Sheinbaum, Mexico has seen gradual erosion of independent institutions — the judiciary, regulatory bodies, and the central bank's effective independence. Macroeconomic stability has been maintained. But analysts are asking whether that stability is real, or whether it rests on foundations that are quietly weakening.

Also worth reading

For students who want to go deeper: the OECD's 2026 Economic Survey of Mexico covers the fiscal deficit, labour informality, and the productivity gap in detail. The Baker Institute at Rice University has also published a useful brief on Mexico's structural growth constraints — search "Locked in Low Gear: Mexico's Struggling Economy."

Questions to think about
Think about these before the session. Come ready to defend a view.
1
Mexico has record foreign investment, a strong peso, and direct access to the US market. Why is economic growth still under 1% a year?
2
More than half of Mexico's workers are in the informal economy. What causes this — and why has no government managed to reverse it?
3
Nearshoring is supposed to be Mexico's big opportunity. What structural barriers might prevent it from transforming the wider economy?
4
The USMCA trade agreement comes up for review in July 2026. What leverage does each side hold, and what are the realistic outcomes?
5
Mexico has maintained macroeconomic stability while democratic institutions have weakened. Is that stability real — or fragile in ways that have not yet surfaced?
6
If you were advising President Sheinbaum, which single structural problem would you prioritise fixing — and why that one above the others?
